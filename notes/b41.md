## Taylor Series
<details class="definition" open><summary>$n$-th degree Taylor polynomial of $f$ at $a$</summary>
$$\begin{align*}
  T_n(x)&=\sum_{i=0}^n\frac{f^{(i)}(a)}{i!}(x-a)^i\\
  &=f(a)+\frac{f'(a)}{1!}(x-a)+\cdots+\frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n
\end{align*}$$

- $T_1(x)$ is tangent line
- $\displaystyle f(x)=\lim_{n\to\infty}T_n(x)$
- $f(x)=T_n(x)+R_n$ 
</details>
<details class="theorem" open><summary>Taylor series</summary>
$$\begin{align*}
  &e^x=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{x^n}{n!}&&\ln(x+1)=\sum_{\color{red}n=1}^\infty(-1)^{n-1}\frac{x^n}n\\
  &\sin x=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1)!}x^{2n+1}&&\cos x=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(-1)^n}{(2n)!}x^{2n}\\
  &(x+1)^a=\sum_{n=0}^\infty\binom anx^n&&\frac1{1+x}=\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n\\
  &\mathrm{atan}(x)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty(1)^n\frac{x^{2n+1}}{2n+1}\\
\end{align*}$$
</details>
<details class="definition" open><summary>$n$th order Taylor series of $f:U\sub\R^k\to\R$ of class $C^{n+1}$ at $a$</summary>
$$\begin{align*}
  T_n(\vec x)&=f(a)+\sum_{i=1}^k h_i\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(a)+\cdots\\
  &+\frac1{n!}\sum_{i_1,\dots,i_n=1}^k h_{i_1}\dots h_{i_n}\frac{\partial^nf}{\partial x_{i_1}\dots\partial x_{i_n}}(a)
\end{align*}$$
</details>

## 3.1 Iterated Partial Derivatives

<details class="definition" open><summary>$f$ of class $C^n$</summary>
$f$ is class $C^n$ 

- iff $f$ is $n$ times continuously diffable 
- iff all $n$th derivative of $f$ is continuous

i.e. e.g. $\displaystyle\frac{\partial^2f}{\partial x^2}=\frac\partial{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right)$ is continuous
</details>
<details class="theorem" open><summary>Mixed Partials</summary>
if $f:\R^2\to\R$ is class $C^2$ then
$$\frac{\partial^2f}{\partial x\partial y}=\frac{\partial^2f}{\partial y\partial x}$$
</details>
<details class="example"><summary>given $z=f(x,y)$ find $\dfrac{\partial^2z}{\partial r^2}$ as polar coordinate</summary>
given $x=r\cos\theta$ and $y=r\sin\theta$

::: {style="font-size: 90%"}

$$\begin{align*}
\therefore\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}&=\nabla f\cdot\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial r}&\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial r}\end{pmatrix}=\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\cos\theta+\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\sin\theta\\
\therefore\frac{\partial^2z}{\partial r^2}&=\frac\partial{\partial r}\left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}\right)=\frac\partial{\partial r}\left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)\cos\theta+\frac\partial{\partial r}\left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\right)\sin\theta\\
\because\frac\partial{\partial r}\left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)&=\nabla\left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)\cdot\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial r}&\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial r}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x^2}&\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x\partial y}\end{pmatrix}\cdot\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial r}&\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial r}\end{pmatrix}\\
&=\frac{\partial z}{\partial x^2}\cos\theta+\frac{\partial z}{\partial x\partial y}\sin\theta\\
\end{align*}$$

:::
</details>

## 2.6 Directional Derivatives
<details class="definition" open><summary>Directional Derivative at $a$ at direction $v$ of $f:\R^n\to\R$ is</summary>
$$D_vf(a)=\nabla f(a)\frac{v}{||v||}]\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(a+hv)-f(a)}h$$
</details>
<details class="definition" open><summary>Properties of Nabla</summary>

**fastest increase**

- if $\nabla f(a)\ne0$
- then $\nabla f(a)$ points to the direction where $f$ is increasing the fastest at $a$

**normal to level surface**

- for $f(x,y,z)=k$
- $\nabla f(a)$ is normal to surface of $f$ at $a$ (by def of tangent plane)
</details>

## 2.5 Properties of Derivative
<details class="theorem" open><summary></summary>
- sum/prod/quot rule
- $D(fg)(x)=g(x)Df(x)+f(x)Dg(x)$
- $df/dx$ means $f$ is **single-variable** fn
- $\partial f/\partial x$ means $f$ is **multi-variable** fn
</details>
<details class="theorem" open><summary>Chain Rule for $f:V\sub\R^k\to\R^m$ and $g:U\sub\R^n\to\R^k$</summary>
$$D(fg)(x)=Df(g(x))Dg(x)\in M^{mn}$$

- case $f:\R^n\to\R$ and $c:\R\to\R^n$, then $fc:\R\to\R$
$$\begin{align*}
  D(fc)&=\frac{df}{dt}=Df(c)Dc=\nabla f(c)\cdot c'\\
  &=\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}&\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y}&\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial z}\end{pmatrix}(c)\cdot\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{dx}{dt}&\dfrac{dy}{dt}&\dfrac{dz}{dt}\end{pmatrix}
\end{align*}$$

- case $f:\R^n\to\R$ and $g:\R^n\to\R^n$, then $fg:\R^n\to\R$
$$\begin{align*}
  D(fg)=Df(g)Dg
\end{align*}$$

- example $f(u(x,y),v(x,y))$
$$\begin{align*}
  \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=Df\cdot\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}&\dfrac{\partial v}{\partial x}\end{pmatrix}=\frac{\partial f}{\partial u}\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial f}{\partial v}\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\\
\end{align*}$$
</details>
<details class="example"><summary>given $z=f(x,y)$ find $\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial r}$ and $\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial\theta}$ as polar coords</summary>
$$\begin{align*}
\because x&=r\cos\theta\quad y=r\sin\theta\\
\therefore\textsf{let }g(r,\theta)&=(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)\\
\therefore D(fg)&=Df(g)Dg=\nabla zDg\\
&=\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x}&\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial y}\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial r}&\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial\theta}\\[1em]\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial r}&\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial\theta}\end{pmatrix}\\
&=\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial x}&\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial y}\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta&-r\sin\theta\\\sin\theta&r\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}
=\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial r}&\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial\theta}\end{pmatrix}\\
\therefore\frac{\partial z}{\partial r}&=\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\cos\theta+\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\sin\theta\\
\frac{\partial z}{\partial\theta}&=\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}r\cos\theta-\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}r\sin\theta\\
\end{align*}$$
</details>
<details class="theorem" open><summary>Differentiability $\implies$ Continuity</summary>
all $\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}$ are continuous
: $\implies f$ is differentiable
: $\implies$ f is continuous and all $\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\exist$

</details>
<details class="example"><summary>$w=w(x^2-y^2,y^2-x^2)\implies y\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}+x\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}=0$</summary>
let $u=x^2-y^2\quad v=y^2-x^2$
$$\begin{align*}
  \therefore w&=w(u,v)\\
  \therefore\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}&=\frac{\partial w}{\partial u}\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial v}\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial w}{\partial u}(2x)+\frac{\partial w}{\partial v}(-2x)\\
  \frac{\partial w}{\partial y}&=\frac{\partial w}{\partial u}\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial w}{\partial v}\frac{\partial v}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial w}{\partial u}(-2y)+\frac{\partial w}{\partial v}(2y)\\
  \therefore y\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}+x\frac{\partial w}{\partial y}&=\frac{\partial w}{\partial u}(2xy)-\frac{\partial w}{\partial v}(2xy)-\frac{\partial w}{\partial u}(2xy)+\frac{\partial w}{\partial v}(2xy)\\
  &=0\\
  \end{align*}$$
</details>

## 2.4 Paths and Curves
> path $c$ is a map, curve $C$ is image of $c$

<details class="definition" open><summary>Paths</summary>
given point $a\in\R^3$ and direction $v\in\R^3$, path of line is
$$c:\R\to\R^3\quad c(t)=a+tv$$

given center $(a,b)\in\R^2$ and radius $r\in\R$

- curve of circle is 
  $$(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2$$
- a path of circle is
  $$c:\R\to\R^2\quad c(t)=r(\cos(t-a),\sin(t-b))$$
</details>

for any path $c$ in $\R^3$
$$c(t)=(x(t),y(t),z(t))$$
for any path $c$ in $\R^2$
$$c(t)=(x(t),y(t))$$

</details>

</details>

## 2.3 Differentiation
<details class="definition" open><summary>Partial Derivative of $f:U\sub \R^n\to\R^m$</summary>
$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\vec x)=f_{x_i}(\vec x)=\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(\vec x+h\vec e_i)-f(\vec x)}h$$
</details>
<details class="definition" open><summary>Jacobian Matrix for $f:U\sub\R^n\to\R^m$</summary>
for $f(x_1,\cdots,x_n)=(f_1(\vec x),\cdots,f_m(\vec x))$
$$Df=\begin{pmatrix}\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}&\cdots&\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}\\\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\\frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1}&\cdots&\frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n}\end{pmatrix}\in M^{mn}$$

$f$ is **differentiable** at $a\in U$ iff
$$\lim_{x\to a}\frac{||f(x)-f(a)-Df(a)(x-a)||}{||x-a||}=0$$
or
$$\forall i,j\enspace\frac{\partial f_i}{\partial x_j}\textsf{ exists and continuous at }a$$

</details>
<details class="definition" open><summary>Gradient of $f:U\sub\R^n\to\R$</summary>
$$Df=\nabla f=\begin{pmatrix}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}&\cdots&\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\end{pmatrix}$$
</details>

<details class="example"><summary>$f:\R^3\to\R^3$ and $g:\R^2\to\R^3$, find $D(fg)(u,v)$</summary>
given
$$\begin{align*}
  f(x,y,z)&=(x+y,xz,yz)\\
  g(u,v)&=(\sin u,\sin v,\cos u+\cos v)\\
\end{align*}$$
then
$$\begin{align*}
  Df(x,y,z)&=\begin{pmatrix}
    \dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial x}&\dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial y}&\dfrac{\partial f_1}{\partial z}\\[1em]
    \dfrac{\partial f_2}{\partial x}&\dfrac{\partial f_2}{\partial y}&\dfrac{\partial f_2}{\partial z}\\[1em]
    \dfrac{\partial f_3}{\partial x}&\dfrac{\partial f_3}{\partial y}&\dfrac{\partial f_3}{\partial z}\\
  \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1&1&0\\z&0&x\\0&z&y\end{pmatrix}\\
  Dg(u,v)&=\begin{pmatrix}
    \dfrac{\partial g_1}{\partial u}&\dfrac{\partial g_1}{\partial v}\\[1em]
    \dfrac{\partial g_2}{\partial u}&\dfrac{\partial g_2}{\partial v}\\[1em]
    \dfrac{\partial g_3}{\partial u}&\dfrac{\partial g_3}{\partial v}\\
  \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} \cos u&0\\0&\cos v\\-\sin u&-\sin v \end{pmatrix}
\end{align*}$$
then 
$$\begin{align*}
  &D(fg)(u,v)=Df(g(u,v))Dg(u,v)\\
  &=\begin{pmatrix}
    1&1&0\\\cos u+\cos v&0&\sin u\\0&\cos u+\cos v&\sin v
  \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}
    \cos u&0\\0&\cos v\\-\sin u&-\sin v
  \end{pmatrix}\\
  &=\begin{pmatrix}
    \cos u&\cos v\\
    \cos2u+\cos u\cos v&-\sin u\sin v\\
    -\sin u\sin v&\cos2v+\cos u\cos v
  \end{pmatrix}
\end{align*}$$
</details>
### Tangent Line
<details class="definition" open><summary>Tangent plane for $z=f(x,y)$ at $a\in\R^2$</summary>
$$z=f(a)+\nabla f(a)(x-a)$$
where $\nabla f(a)$ is **normal**
</details>
<details class="example"><summary>Tangent plane of $z=x^2+y^4+e^{xy}$ at $a=(1,0,2)$</summary>
$$\begin{align*}
  \nabla z&=\begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}&\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 2x+ye^{xy}&4y^3+xe^{xy} \end{pmatrix}\\
\therefore z&=2+\nabla z(1,0)\begin{pmatrix} x-1\\y-0 \end{pmatrix}
  =2+\begin{pmatrix}2&1\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x-1\\y \end{pmatrix}\\
  &=2(x-1)+y+2=2x+y\\
\end{align*}$$
$\therefore$ tangent plane is $z=2x+y$
</details>
<details class="example"><summary>$f(x,y)=x\ln(xy)$ approx $f(1.98,0.4)$ from $a=(2,1/2)$</summary>
$$\begin{align*}
    \because f&=x\ln x+x\ln y\\
    \therefore\nabla f&=\begin{pmatrix}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}&\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}\ln(xy)+1&\frac xy\end{pmatrix}\\
    \therefore z&=f(a)+\nabla f(a)(x-a)=\begin{pmatrix}1&4\end{pmatrix}\cdot\begin{pmatrix}x-2\\y-1/2\end{pmatrix}\\
    &=x+4y-4\\
    \therefore f(1.98,0.4)&\approx z(1.98,0.4)=-0.42\\
\end{align*}$$
</details>
<details class="definition" open><summary>Tangent plane for $f(x,y,z)=k$ at $a\in\R^3$</summary>
$$\nabla f(a)(x-a)=0$$

where $\nabla f(a)$ is **normal**
</details>
<details class="example"><summary>Tangent plane of $e^x+z\sin y=2$ at $a=(0,\pi/2,1)$</summary>
$$\begin{align*}
  \because\nabla f&=\begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}&\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}&\frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}e^x&2\cos y&\sin y\end{pmatrix}\\
  \therefore\nabla f(a)&=\begin{pmatrix}1&0&1\end{pmatrix}\\
  \therefore\nabla f(a)(x-a)&=\begin{pmatrix}1&0&1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}x\\y-\pi/2\\z-1\end{pmatrix}=x+z-1=0\\
  \therefore 0&=x+z-1\\
\end{align*}$$
</details>
<details class="example"><summary>find $a$ on $\small x^2+2y^2+3z^2=1$ with plane $||$ to $\small 3x-y+3z=1$</summary>
$\because$ normal of $3x-y+3z=1$ is $(3,-1,3)$<br>
$\therefore$ normal of tangent plane is $r(3,-1,3)$ for $r\in\R$
$$\begin{align*}
  \therefore\exist r\in\R\textsf{ st }\nabla f&=r(3,-1,3)\\
  \therefore\nabla f(a)&=\begin{pmatrix}2x\\4y\\6z\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}3r\\-r\\3r\\\end{pmatrix}\\
  \therefore\begin{cases} 2x=3r\\ 4y=-r\\ 6z=3r\\ \end{cases}&\implies\begin{cases} x=3r/2\\ y=-r/4\\ z=r/2\\ \end{cases}\\
  \because x^2+2y^2+3z^2&=1\\
  \therefore (3r/2)^2+2(-r/4)^2+3(r/2)^2&=1\implies r=\pm\frac{2\sqrt2}5\\
  \therefore a=r(3,-1,3)\quad r&=\pm\frac{2\sqrt2}5\\
\end{align*}$$

</details>

\end{align*}$$

  \therefore0<||(x,y,z)||<\delta\implies\left|\frac{x^2y^2z^2}{x^2+y^2+z^2}\right|&\le(x^2+y^2+z^2)^2\\ &<\delta^2=\varepsilon\quad\blacksquare
\end{align*}$$
</details>
<details class="example"><summary>use **upper bound** for $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)}xy=ab$</summary>
wts $\forall\varepsilon>0\enspace\exist\delta>0$ st 
$$0<\sqrt{(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2}<\delta\implies|xy-ab|<\varepsilon$$

$$\begin{align*}
  \because |y-b|&\le\sqrt{(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2}<\delta\\
  \therefore|y|-|b|&\le|y-b|<\delta\le1\\
  \therefore|y|&\le|b|+1\\
  \\
  \therefore|xy-ab|&=|xy-ay+ay-ab|=|x-a||y|+|a||y-b|\\
  &\le|x-a|(|b|+1)+|y-b||a|\\
  &\le\sqrt{(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2}(|a|+|b|+1)\\
  &\le(|a|+|b|+1)\delta=\varepsilon
\end{align*}$$

proof: given $\varepsilon>0$ let $\delta=\textrm{min}(1,\varepsilon/(1+|a|+|b|))>0$

</details>
<details class="example"><summary>use **$x=ky$** for $\displaystyle\lim_{v\to0}\frac{5y^4\cos^2x}{x^4+y^4}$ DNE</summary>

<details class="example"><summary>use **sandwich** for $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(1^+,0)}\frac{(x-1)^2\ln x}{(x-1)^2+y^2}=0$</summary>

\end{align*}$$

## 1.3 Cross Products
<details class="definition" open><summary>$a=(a_1,a_2,a_3)\quad b=(b_1,b_2,b_3)$</summary>
$$a\times b=\begin{vmatrix}e_1&e_2&e_3\\a_1&a_2&a_3\\b_1&b_2&b_3\end{vmatrix}$$
where $e_i$ is standard basis
</details>
<details class="example"><summary>let $p$ be plane through `a(0, 2, 1) b(1, -1, -1) c(1, 4, 1)`</summary>
- find equation of $p$
$$\begin{align*}
  \because b-a&=(1,-3,-2)\quad c-a=(1,2,0)\textsf{ are vectors on }p\\
  \therefore n&=(1,-3,-2)\times(1,2,0)=\begin{vmatrix}e_1&e_2&e_3\\1&-3&-2\\1&2&0\end{vmatrix}\\
  &=4e_1-2e_2+5e_3=(4,-2,5)\\
  \because n\cdot a&=-4+5=1\\
  \therefore 4x-2y+5z&=1\textsf{ is the equation}
\end{align*}$$
</details>

## Lines and Planes
<details class="definition" open><summary>Equation of a Line</summary>
line $l$ passes $a\in\R^n$ at direction $v\in\R^n$ is (**Point-Direction**)
$$l=a+tv\quad t\in\R$$

line $l$ passes $a\in\R^n$ and $b\in\R^n$ is (**Point-Point**)
$$l=a+(b-a)t\quad t\in\R$$
</details>
<details class="definition" open><summary>Equation of a Plane</summary>
plane with basis $\{v,w\}\sube\R^3$ and passes $a\in\R^3$ is
$$p=a+sv+tw\quad s,t\in\R$$

plane has normal $n\in\R^3$ and passes $a\in\R^3$ is
$$n\cdot(x-a)=0\qquad n\cdot x=n\cdot a\quad x\in\R^3$$
$$ax+by+cz=d$$
</details>
<details class="theorem" open><summary>Angle Between Vectors & Planes</summary>
for $a,b\in\R^3$, angle between vectors is $\theta\in[0,\pi]$ st
$$a\cdot b=||a||||b||\cos\theta$$

- $a\cdot b=0\implies\perp$
- $\exist r\in\R\enspace a=rb\implies\parallel$

for planes with normals $n,m\in\R^3$, angle between planes is
<center>angle between normals</center>
</details>

<details class="example"><summary>$r^2+r(2\sin\theta-6\cos\theta)=0$ to cartesian</summary>
let $x=r\cos\theta\quad y=r\sin\theta$
$$\begin{align*}
  \therefore r^2+r(2\sin\theta-6\cos\theta)&=r^2+2r\sin\theta-6r\cos\theta\\
  &=x^2+y^2+2y-6x=0\\
  \therefore (x^2-6x+9)+(y^2+2y+1)-10&=0\\
  \therefore (x-3)^2+(y+1)^2&=10
\end{align*}$$
$\therefore$ the curve is a circle centered at $(3,-1)$ with radius $\sqrt{10}$
</details>
<details class="example"><summary>$x^2+y^2=4$ to spherical</summary>
let $x=\rho\sin\varphi\cos\theta\quad y=\rho\sin\varphi\sin\theta\quad z=\rho\cos\varphi$
$$\begin{align*}
  \therefore x^2+y^2&=\rho^2\sin^2\varphi(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta)=4\\
\rho^2\sin^2\varphi&=4\\
\rho\sin\varphi&=2\\
\therefore\rho&=2/\sin\varphi\\
\end{align*}$$
</details>
